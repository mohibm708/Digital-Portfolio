1.1.1:

After 5 runs, 100 darts returned π-estimates of 3.16, 3.28, 3.24, 3.36, and 3.12.


1.1.2:

After 5 runs, 1000 darts returned π-estimates of 3.228, 3.128, 3.164, 3.204, and 3.124.


1.2:

The estimates from 1000 darts are more specific than the estimates from 100 darts.


1.3:

If N is 10000, the program will usually return values with five significant figures (i.e. 3.1528, 3.148, 3.1132, 3.1532 and 3.1396).


1.4:

If the darts are deliberately thrown outside or inside the circle, the π-estimate will (respectively) be lower or higher than the actual value. If the darts are thrown purely randomly, the alignment of the estimate with π is completely dependent on the probability that a dart will land within the circle (the ratio of the circle and square's areas multiplied by 100; this is constantly ~78.53% given that the square's length and width are the circle's radius doubled).


What did I learn? What is the "big idea"?

I learned how to use random numbers to find a fundamental constant in math. The big idea of this experiment entails the further comprehension of randomization's importance.


What challenges did I encounter?

Thinking about how to devise π without actually using it to construct the circle proved somewhat difficult until I realized that there were only a few components of the circle that were needed to sort dart positions.


How could this experience be improved?

I believe this simulation or some alternative to it could help with understanding how making the estimate is done.


Free Reflection: How has what I've learned affected my thinking?

I've got a better understanding of how to use randoms to my advantage, and will definitely use it in other Swift ventures.
